## Regulatory Context Summary

PRP/PRF preparation systems are FDA-regulated as Class II medical devices and are cleared via the 510(k) pathway. They are sold direct-to-licensed-practitioner without a separate Rx, because the autologous biologic itself (the patient's own blood) is not an FDA-regulated drug — only the device that processes it is regulated. Compatible centrifuges, collection tubes, anticoagulants (sodium citrate, ACD-A), activators (10% calcium chloride), and blunt cannulas are all in scope for a B2B catalog selling to NPs, PAs, MDs, and DOs ([Dr. PRP USA 510(k) clearance summary](https://www.drprpusa.com/dr-prp-usa-receives-fda-510k-clearance-for-its-prp-kits.html); [EmCyte PurePRP SupraPhysiologic clearance, BK190317](https://www.biospace.com/emcyte-announces-fda-510-k-clearance-for-its-pureprp-supraphysiologic-concentrating-system)).

The April 2026 FDA peptide reclassification covered in Part 1 is irrelevant to PRP/PRF — those rules govern compounded synthetic peptide APIs, not autologous platelet products. PRP/PRF systems are completely insulated from the PCAC review schedule. The one peripheral regulatory concern is the ZEO ScientifiX **Patient Pure X™ (PPX™)** platelet-derived exosome service, treated as a special case below: PPX is autologous (patient's own blood, returned as a concentrated EV fraction), which means it sidesteps the state-level stem cell laws (Florida SB 1768, Utah equivalent) that govern allogeneic regenerative biologics. PPX can be sold nationwide without geographic restriction.

Transdermal peptide patches sit in a more fragile regulatory position. Patches that deliver **GHK-Cu for cosmetic skin claims** (firmness, fine lines, post-procedure recovery) are defensible under the OTC topical/cosmetic regulatory regime, with published in-vitro skin-penetration data showing clinically meaningful dermal delivery ([Hostynek 2010, Human skin penetration of a copper tripeptide](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3016279/)). Patches that claim **systemic delivery of BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295, or MOTS-C** carry no published pharmacokinetic data in humans and exist almost entirely as "research-use" SKUs with disclaimers. The 2026 sports-medicine literature is explicit: "there is no evidence from randomized trials in humans that any of these peptides provide the benefits that are advocated" via non-injectable routes ([Mayfield et al., AJSM 2026](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/03635465251357593)). This narrows what we can responsibly carry — see Category C summary for the recommendation.

## Category B Summary

Gold-standard systems in 2026 fall into two camps: **EmCyte PurePRP** (dual-syringe, highest-concentration) and **Harvest SmartPrep 3** (legacy premium, also does BMAC and AdiPrep) on the high-concentration end; **RegenLab RegenKit THT** and **Selphyl PRFM** on the simpler/single-spin end. **Choukroun Duo Quattro** is the unambiguous PRF (vs. PRP) gold standard — there is no real competitor for clinically validated PRF protocols. Distribution shifts worth flagging: **Harvest Technologies** was acquired by Terumo and then by **Globus Medical** ([Globus Medical Harvest product page](https://www.globusmedical.com/products/harvest/)) — practices buying SmartPrep should source through Globus, not legacy Terumo channels. **Arthrex Angel** is a legitimate alternative on the orthopedic-leaning side ([Arthrex Angel system page](https://aesthetics.arthrex.com/en/products/angel-system)) but is less common in pure aesthetic use. **"Arkeo"** appeared in the original research priority list but I could not verify the brand against any 2026 distribution channel — it is either misspelled, regional/non-U.S., or discontinued; do not include in the catalog without further confirmation. The catalog recommendation is to anchor on EmCyte (centrifuge + kit), add Choukroun for PRF practices, stock RegenKit THT as the simpler-workflow alternative, carry T-LAB PRF tubes for centrifuge-agnostic PRF, and offer the PPX service as the premium autologous-EV upsell. Selphyl is duplicative of RegenKit at this scope and is not recommended in the initial Category B launch.

## Category C — Transdermal Peptide Patches

### Strong Caution Up Front

The transdermal peptide patch category is, in 2026, dominated by products with weak-to-nonexistent published pharmacokinetic evidence in humans. Patches that claim to deliver **systemic-acting peptides** (BPC-157, TB-500, CJC-1295, MOTS-C) through intact skin without iontophoresis or microneedle assistance face the basic problem that those peptides are large, hydrophilic, and charged molecules that do not cross the stratum corneum at clinically meaningful rates ([Strategies for transportation of peptides across the skin, 2025 review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11703487/)). The single exception with credible in-vitro skin penetration data is **GHK-Cu**, which is small, lipophilic enough to penetrate the dermis, and has a defensible cosmetic/topical regulatory positioning ([Hostynek 2010 in-vitro penetration](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3016279/); [Pickart 2018 GHK-Cu regenerative review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6073405/)).

Two SKUs below are recommended for the catalog with constraints. The remainder are flagged as either MLM-channel (incompatible with a clinical positioning) or research-use-labeled (incompatible with FDA-defensible B2B sale).

### Excluded — Documented Reasons

#### LifeWave X39 (Excluded)

LifeWave's X39 patch is **explicitly not transdermal** — LifeWave's own materials state "no drugs, chemicals, or other substances pass into the body" and that the mechanism is reflected infrared phototherapy intended to elevate endogenous GHK-Cu production ([LifeWave X39 study](https://lifewave.com/Content/images/home/science/pdf/Experimental-Study-of-Lifewave-X-39-patches-report-Final-draft-3.pdf)). The supporting clinical evidence is sponsor-funded with small samples and has been criticized in the independent regenerative-medicine literature ([The Niche / Knoepfler critique, 2023](https://ipscell.com/2023/02/lifewave-x39-stem-cell-patch-story-has-holes/)). LifeWave's distribution model is multi-level marketing. **Do not carry.** Carrying an MLM-distributed phototherapy patch in a clinical-platform catalog would damage ATG's credibility with the licensed-practitioner segment we are targeting.

#### TB-500 / BPC-157 Combination Patches (Excluded for now)

Multiple vendors (Pure Peptides "Wolverine," Biomediceuticals, Micropharma) sell TB-500/BPC-157 combination patches under "research use only" labeling. TB-500 is on the FDA Category 2 / PCAC review schedule for July 23, 2026 ([Part 1 regulatory section]). Carrying a research-use-labeled patch with a peptide currently under active FDA compounding review is a category we cannot defend in 2026. **Revisit after the July 2026 PCAC ruling** — if BPC-157/TB-500 are formally accepted to the 503A bulks list, the compounded-injectable pathway will dominate practitioner demand and the patches will be a niche take-home format that may then be appropriate.

#### CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin Patches (Excluded)

CJC-1295 has no published transdermal pharmacokinetic data. Patches claiming systemic GH-axis effects via passive skin diffusion of a 30+ amino-acid peptide are not pharmacologically credible. **Do not carry.**

#### MOTS-C, Semax, Selank Patches (Excluded)

Same pattern as CJC-1295 — no credible pharmacokinetic basis for systemic delivery via passive transdermal patch. Semax and Selank also carry the U.S. legal-status concern flagged in Part 1 (sold as "research chemicals," not legitimately compliant supplements). **Do not carry.**

#### "Corganix" and "Trevena Health" (Not found)

Neither brand surfaced in 2026 distribution searches. Either misnamed in the original research brief, regional/non-U.S., or no longer trading. Not pursued further.

## Category C Summary

Honest read: the transdermal peptide patch category in 2026 is mostly noise. The legitimate signal narrows to (1) **GHK-Cu cosmetic patches** with published in-vitro penetration data and a defensible topical regulatory position, and (2) the **Peptual iontophoretic multi-peptide patch** as a higher-priced practitioner-distributed take-home, with explicit messaging caveats around the BPC-157 component. The recommendation is to launch Category C with these two SKUs, position both as adjunctive/cosmetic rather than therapeutic, and explicitly exclude LifeWave (MLM/credibility risk), the systemic-claim BPC-157/TB-500 patches (regulatory risk pending July 2026 PCAC ruling), and the CJC-1295/MOTS-C/Semax/Selank patches (no pharmacological basis, in some cases legal-status problems). Revisit the excluded SKUs in Q3 2026 after the FDA peptide compounding decisions are public.
