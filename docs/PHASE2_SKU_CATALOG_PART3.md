## Regulatory Context Summary

Two distinct regulatory regimes govern the products in this category. First, the **federal tissue regime**: perinatal-derived biologics (extracellular vesicles, stem-cell preparations, amniotic membrane) are classified by FDA as Human Cells, Tissues, and Cellular and Tissue-Based Products (HCT/Ps). Products that meet the criteria in 21 CFR 1271.10 — minimally manipulated, intended for homologous use, not combined with a drug or device, and not dependent on the metabolic activity of living cells for their primary function — are regulated solely under **Section 361** of the Public Health Service Act and do not require an FDA approval to be marketed. Anything that fails any of those four prongs is a Section 351 biologic and requires a Biologics License Application (BLA) or active IND. Most perinatal EV/exosome and stem-cell preparations as currently sold are, in FDA's view, **Section 351** products being marketed without BLAs — the regulatory gap the state legislation below is attempting to occupy. Amniotic membrane sheets used as a structural wound covering are the most defensible 361 HCT/P in this category ([BioTissue 361 HCT/P framing](https://biotissue.com/about-us/our-company/)).

Second, the **state regenerative-medicine regime**. Florida's **Senate Bill 1768**, signed into law and effective July 1, 2025, permits licensed Florida physicians to perform stem-cell therapies in orthopedics, wound care, and pain management using allogeneic sources (umbilical-cord blood, adult stem cells, ethically obtained HCT/Ps) — but **not** fetal or embryonic tissue. Providers must disclose that the therapy is not FDA-approved, obtain written patient consent, and source from American labs accredited by NMDP, WMDA, AABB, or AATB. The bill passed 37–0 in the Senate and 112–0 in the House ([SB 1768 full text](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2025/1768); [FL House summary](https://www.flhouse.gov/Sections/Bills/billsdetail.aspx?BillId=82252); [Florida Politics coverage](https://floridapolitics.com/archives/748132-florida-expands-access-to-regenerative-medicine-with-new-stem-cell-therapy-law/); [Holland & Knight insight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/08/new-florida-law-attempts-to-provide-leeway-for-certain-stem-cell)). **Utah SB 199**, effective May 1, 2024, permits placental/perinatal stem-cell therapies with analogous informed-consent requirements — but Utah's scope-of-provider is broader (NPs, PAs, naturopaths, chiropractors, podiatrists, nurses) and lacks Florida's sourcing-accreditation mandate ([Utah SB 199 summary](https://www.billtrack50.com/billdetail/1831386); [Science/AAAS coverage](https://www.science.org/content/article/utah-flouts-fda-law-greenlighting-placental-stem-cell-therapies); [Celmedica state guide](https://celmedica.com/state-legal-guides/stem-cell-regenerative-medicine-in-utah-whats-legal-and-what-to-know/)). **Nevada** has had parallel language on the books longer. **Texas** HB 810 (2017, "Charlie's Law") predates both but is narrower — it covers investigational stem-cell use for terminal patients only and does not provide the aesthetic/wellness/orthopedic runway the Florida and Utah laws create ([Allied Pain Texas summary](https://alliedwaco.com/is-stem-cell-therapy-legal-in-texas/); [The Niche trend piece](https://ipscell.com/2025/04/florida-stem-cell-bill-is-part-of-state-trend-to-sidestep-fda/)). No additional 2026 state legislation has passed to date; Arizona's HB 2121 (2019) died in committee.

The practical result for ATG Clinical Supplies: **allogeneic perinatal-derived biologics (DayZero EV, DayZero Stem Cells, ZEO GROW X component, amniotic membrane sheets) can be shipped to Florida and Utah providers without regulatory controversy**. Shipping the same SKUs to providers in other states exposes the manufacturer (and potentially ATG as distributor) to FDA enforcement under Section 351. The autologous **Patient Pure X™ (PPX™)** service — already covered in detail in Part 2 under Category B — is the exception: because the raw material is the patient's own blood, it sidesteps the HCT/P allogeneic framework entirely and can be ordered by any licensed provider nationwide.

Regen Therapy is **not a clinic**. Public materials describe it as a research/education/MSO advisory layer serving a claimed 3,000 clinics and 24,000 practitioners. **Direct competition:** biologics supply to aesthetic and longevity-oriented practices. If ATG's Clinical Supplies catalog and Regen Therapy's clinic network compete for the same provider's biologics spend, the overlap is real.

The April 9, 2026 language is specific: ZEO is "Regen Therapy's exclusive partner for biologic research, development, and manufacturing of biologic therapeutics." That phrasing constrains **Regen Therapy's** sourcing — Regen cannot contract a competing biologics manufacturer for its 24,000-practitioner network — but it does **not** on its face forbid ZEO from selling directly to non-Regen-Therapy physician accounts. This is the most important strategic question for ATG and should be **verified directly with ZEO business development** before building a catalog integration; the MOU text itself is not fully public.

### Question 5 — ATG differentiation opportunities

Regen Therapy's stack is Rx-centric (telehealth + pharmacy fulfillment + longevity peptide injectables via licensed prescribers), education-heavy, and primary-care/longevity-clinic positioned. ATG's differentiation live within that gap:

1. **Aesthetic injector focus.** Regen's positioning is longevity / wellness / primary-care; ATG's is injector (PA/NP/MD/DO) running actual aesthetic procedures. Different buyer, different shelf.
2. **Equipment plus consumables plus skincare actives in one catalog.** Regen doesn't sell Celluma or Dr. Pen pens or SkinCeuticals CE Ferulic; ATG does.
3. **Soft-gated direct-ship catalog model without Rx overhead.** Regen's Stealth Health layer means every patient touchpoint runs through a prescriber/telehealth visit. ATG's model is lower friction for products that don't require a prescriber visit (PRP kits, topical exosomes, OTC peptide supplements).
4. **No 3,000-clinic network obligation.** A provider who wants to buy one EmCyte kit and one BENEV ERC bottle doesn't need to join a 24,000-practitioner platform to do it.
5. **Mobile practice / founding-provider equity / software tooling.** ATG's broader positioning (EHR + AI Simulator + Clinical Supplies) offers integration story Regen doesn't have.

The clearest differentiation is: **ATG is a clinical-operating-system platform; Regen Therapy is a longevity-medicine network.** Both can coexist.

### Question 7 — Mitochondrial / microRNA narrative: science vs. marketing

**The real science:** extracellular vesicles (exosomes) do carry microRNA cargo, and specific miRNAs — including miR-146a (inflammation modulation), miR-21 (osteocyte/senescence signaling), and miR-302b (cellular reprogramming) — have documented roles in cellular senescence and aging pathways. miR-146a is demonstrably anti-inflammatory and downregulated in aged immune cells; miR-21 is downregulated in aged bone and dermal fibroblasts; miR-302b has shown preclinical lifespan-extension effects in mice ([Exosomes and microRNAs in Aging, PMC8393989](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8393989/); [miR-302b in Cell Metabolism](https://www.cell.com/cell-metabolism/fulltext/S1550-4131(24)00481-9); [miR-146a umbilical-cord MSC exosomes, Aging journal](https://www.aging-us.com/article/202466/text); [Role of miRNA in Skin Aging, PMC7432402](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7432402/)). PGC-1α and TFAM are genuine mitochondrial master regulators.

**The marketing extension:** the leap from "these miRNAs and master regulators exist and do real things in cells" to "administering perinatal exosome preparations reprograms human cellular aging" is **not supported by randomized human clinical trials**. Small-molecule and perinatal-EV studies in humans are almost entirely open-label, small-n, or animal-model. No exosome product on the U.S. market today has demonstrated clinically meaningful lifespan or healthspan extension in a well-controlled human study. The "aging is a communication problem" framing is rhetorically effective and has real mechanistic support, but the implied efficacy claim for *this* product class at *this* dose *delivered this way* runs well ahead of the evidence base.

Honest read: **the biology is real; the clinical-scale efficacy claim is promotional.** ATG should feel free to carry ZEO products for providers who want them, but should not repeat the "DNA-level cellular therapy" framing in its own marketing. Stick to structure/function and procedure-adjunct claims that the evidence base supports.

### Question 8 — Strategic recommendation on ZEO partnership

Three options, ordered by risk.

**Option A (conservative, recommended for catalog launch):** Carry **PPX™ (autologous, nationwide), ZEO GROW X (topical, nationwide), and amniotic membrane sheets (361 HCT/P, nationwide)** at launch. Defer **DayZero EV and DayZero Stem Cells** until Phase 3 architecture exists (geo-gating, provider license verification against FL/UT, informed-consent template integration, and dry-ice fulfillment workflow). Open a ZEO account and attend Masterclass to establish the relationship and validate MOU-exclusivity read from Question 4, but do not list the geo-restricted SKUs on the public catalog. This option gets ATG ~80% of the commercial value with ~20% of the regulatory/logistics risk.

**Option B (moderate):** Include DayZero EV and DayZero Stem Cells in the catalog with hard geo-gating — providers outside FL/UT cannot even see the SKU on the unlocked gate. Build the logistics and disclosure flow to support the full portfolio. This matches the original CLAUDE.md scope for Category D but requires the Phase 3 architecture (mainly the state-license verification) that isn't built yet.

**Option C (aggressive):** Attempt to become a ZEO "physician reseller network for aesthetic providers," parallel to Regen Therapy's broader longevity network. This requires (a) written confirmation from ZEO that the MOU does not preclude a parallel aesthetic-specialty distribution agreement, (b) ATG building out the Masterclass-equivalent credentialing layer, and (c) capital commitment to cold-chain and consent-workflow infrastructure. Higher upside; substantially higher build cost; direct competitive response from Regen Therapy is likely. Do not attempt before Phase 3.

**Recommendation: Option A for Phase 2 launch.** Revisit Option B once Phase 3 geo-gating and license-verification are in place, and keep Option C as a Q4 2026 conversation with ZEO business development once the Hims & Hers longevity launch reshapes the competitive landscape.
